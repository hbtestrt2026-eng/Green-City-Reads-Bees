Why Vinyl Records Are Outselling CDs Again — And What It Means for Music

By James Okafor · June 18, 2026 · 5 min read

🎵

For the third consecutive year, vinyl records have outsold CDs in the United States. According to the Recording Industry Association of America, vinyl sales hit $1.8 billion in 2025, while CD revenue fell to $570 million — a reversal that would have seemed absurd just a decade ago. The last time vinyl dominated physical music sales this decisively was 1987.

"People want something they can hold, something with weight," says Priya Sharma, owner of Groove District Records in Austin, Texas. "Streaming is convenient, but it's invisible. Vinyl is a ritual."

The demographics driving the boom might surprise skeptics. While nostalgia plays a role among Gen X and Boomer collectors, the fastest-growing segment of vinyl buyers is 18-to-24-year-olds. A 2025 survey by MusicWatch found that 38% of Gen Z vinyl purchasers don't even own a turntable — they buy records as cultural artifacts, display pieces, and tangible connections to artists they love.

"The vinyl resurgence isn't really about audio quality. It's about intentionality. In an age of infinite choice and algorithmic playlists, putting on a record is a deliberate act. You're choosing to listen to one thing, start to finish." — Dr. Marcus Bellingham, Cultural Studies, NYU

The supply side has struggled to keep pace. There are currently only about 50 vinyl pressing plants operating worldwide, down from hundreds in the 1970s. Lead times for new pressings can stretch to six months or more, and major labels often crowd out independent artists for pressing capacity. United Record Pressing in Nashville — the largest plant in the U.S. — completed a $55 million expansion in 2024, adding 16 new presses to meet demand.

The economics are compelling for artists too. A vinyl record typically retails for $25-40 and offers significantly better margins than streaming, where artists earn fractions of a cent per play. For independent musicians, a limited vinyl pressing of 500 copies can generate more revenue than millions of streams.

Retailers are adapting. Barnes & Noble has tripled its vinyl floor space since 2022. Target and Walmart now stock curated vinyl selections. And the number of independent record stores in the U.S. has grown for seven consecutive years, reaching 1,400 in 2025 — still far from the 1990s peak of 3,500, but a meaningful reversal of a decades-long decline.

Not everyone is celebrating. Environmental critics point out that vinyl records are made from PVC, a petroleum-based plastic that is difficult to recycle and releases toxic compounds during manufacturing. Several startups are developing bio-based alternatives, but none have achieved the sound quality and durability of traditional PVC pressings at commercial scale.

Whether the vinyl boom represents a permanent shift or a cultural moment remains debated. But for now, the crackle of a needle hitting a groove continues to resonate with a generation that grew up never knowing what it sounded like.
